Canopy Energy Balance

This page documents the canopy energy module used in ClimaLand's vegetation standalone runs. The module closes the energy balance of a (two‑leaf) canopy by solving for leaf temperature(s) and fluxes of sensible and latent heat given radiative forcing and aerodynamic/stomatal constraints.

It corresponds to src/standalone/Vegetation/canopy_energy.jl and integrates with radiative transfer, stomatal conductance/photosynthesis, and the surface/hydrology modules.

Summary

  • Purpose: solve canopy energy balance and return leaf (sunlit/shaded) temperatures and turbulent fluxes.
  • Inputs: meteorology (air temperature, pressure, humidity, wind), radiation (shortwave, longwave; direct/diffuse), canopy structure (LAI, clumping), conductances (aerodynamic, boundary‑layer, stomatal), and available water for transpiration.
  • Core: two‑leaf formulation (sunlit & shaded) that computes net radiation, solves the energy balance for leaf temperature(s), and computes sensible (H) and latent (LE) heat using Penman–Monteith‑consistent relations.
  • Outputs: leaf temperatures, H, LE, canopy net radiation, and supporting diagnostics (e.g., conductances, VPD at the leaf).

Model formulation

Net radiation per leaf class

For each class c ∈ {sun, shade}:

\[R_{n,c} = SW_{abs,c} + LW_{abs,c} - \epsilon\sigma\,T_{l,c}^4,\]

where SW_{abs,c} and LW_{abs,c} are the absorbed shortwave and longwave fluxes, \epsilon is leaf emissivity, \sigma the Stefan–Boltzmann constant, and T_{l,c} the leaf temperature (K).

Turbulent fluxes

Sensible heat:

\[H_c = \rho\,c_p\,g_{H,c}\,(T_{l,c} - T_a),\]

Latent heat (bulk formulation consistent with Penman–Monteith):

\[LE_c = \lambda\,g_{w,c}\,(q^*(T_{l,c}) - q_a),\]

with air density \rho, heat capacity c_p, latent heat of vaporization \lambda, aerodynamic+boundary layer conductance to heat g_{H,c}, to water vapour g_{w,c} (including stomatal pathway), saturation specific humidity at leaf surface q^*(T_{l,c}), and ambient specific humidity q_a.

Energy balance closure

The energy balance for each class is

\[R_{n,c} = H_c + LE_c + S_c,\]

where S_c is a (usually small) storage/biochemical term; often S_c≈0 at model timesteps ≥ 10–30 min. The module solves for T_{l,c} (and hence H_c, LE_c) using either a Newton iteration or an analytic linearization if enabled. Canopy‑scale fluxes are the LAI‑weighted sum of the two classes.

Numerical details & coupling

  • Radiation: SW_{abs,c}/LW_{abs,c} come from the canopy RT module (direct/diffuse split, view factors).
  • Stomata/photosynthesis: g_s is provided by the stomatal model (e.g., Medlyn, Ball–Berry) and may be down‑regulated by soil‑moisture stress modules.
  • Hydrology: Transpiration draws from plant‑available water; optional limiters cap LE when supply is constrained.
  • Aerodynamics: g_H/g_w follow micrometeorological parameterizations (e.g., Monin–Obukhov with within‑canopy decay). Boundary‑layer conductance scales with wind and leaf characteristic length.
  • Solver: The nonlinearity enters via q^*(T_l) and LW ∝ T_l^4. The solver iterates until energy residuals fall below tolerance.

Using the canopy energy module in a standalone run

using ClimaLand

energy = Vegetation.CanopyEnergy.Config(
    two_leaf = true,
    solver = :newton,
    # other options: stability corrections, floors, etc.
)

veg = Vegetation.CanopyConfig(
    energy = energy,
    # stomata/photosynthesis, RT, etc.
)

model = ClimaLand.Standalone.CanopyModel(vegetation = veg)
sol = ClimaLand.solve!(model; tspan = (0.0, 86400.0))

d = ClimaLand.diagnostics(sol)
Tsun  = d[:Tleaf_sun]     # K
Tshade= d[:Tleaf_shade]   # K
H     = d[:H_canopy]      # W m^-2
LE    = d[:LE_canopy]     # W m^-2
Rn    = d[:Rn_canopy]     # W m^-2

Output diagnostics

  • :Tleaf_sun, :Tleaf_shade — leaf temperatures (K)
  • :H_canopy, :LE_canopy, :Rn_canopy — canopy sensible, latent, and net radiation (W m⁻²)
  • :gH_sun, :gH_shade, :gw_sun, :gw_shade — conductances used (m s⁻¹)
  • :VPD_leaf_sun, :VPD_leaf_shade — leaf‑level VPD (Pa)
  • Optional supply/lambda diagnostics if hydrological limiters are active

Tips

  • Ensure the time step is adequate for convergence during rapid transients (morning spin‑up, cloud edges).
  • Use consistent units when converting g_s (mol m⁻² s⁻¹) to g_w (m s⁻¹) — account for temperature/pressure.
  • If the canopy is extremely sparse or dense, check LAI partitioning and within‑canopy wind attenuation settings.
  • Verify that hydrological constraints (soil/plant hydraulics) are not silently capping LE if you expect high transpiration.
